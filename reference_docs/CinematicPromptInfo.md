**The key is to layer your prompts with specific photographic and directorial details. Here is a breakdown of the essential characteristics and directions to use.**

**The "Cinematic Recipe": A Four-Part Structure\[3]**

**To get the best results, structure your prompt by layering these four crucial elements. Think of it as building a scene from the ground up.**

**1. The Subject and Emotional Direction (The "Who \& Why")**

**Don't just describe physical features. "Direct" your subject to convey a specific mood or story.\[3] A face without emotion feels like a snapshot, not cinema.**

**Direct the Emotion: Instead of "a portrait of a man," try: "a candid portrait of a weary man with a thousand-yard stare," or "a joyful woman laughing with her head thrown back, eyes crinkled."**

**Specify the Action: Give them something to do or react to. "Looking out a rain-streaked window," "pausing in mid-conversation," or "taking a drag from a cigarette."**

**Keywords for Realism: candid, authentic moment, unguarded expression, raw emotion, documentary style, street photography portrait.**

**2. The Environment and Composition (The "Where \& How")**

**The setting and how you frame the shot are vital for storytelling.\[3]**

**Build an Atmosphere: The background should add to the story.\[3] "In a smoky, dimly lit jazz club," "on a bustling Tokyo street at night," or "in a sun-drenched, dusty apartment."**

**Use Compositional Rules: Guide the AI on how to frame the image.**

**Rule of thirds: Places the subject off-center for a more dynamic look.\[3]**

**Shallow depth of field or bokeh: Blurs the background to isolate the subject, a hallmark of cinematic portraiture.\[3]**

**Framing: Use elements in the foreground to create depth, like "shot through a doorway" or "over-the-shoulder shot."**

**3. Lighting Direction (The "Mood Maker")**

**Lighting is perhaps the most critical element.\[3] Never leave it to chance.**

**Natural Light: Golden hour (warm, low sun), blue hour (twilight), window light (soft and directional), overcast light (soft and even).**

**Dramatic \& Artificial Light: Chiaroscuro or Rembrandt lighting (high contrast with deep shadows), neon lighting (colorful, urban feel), practical lights (light sources visible on screen, like lamps).**

**Direction: "Side-lit to accentuate textures," "backlit to create a rim light halo," or "top-down lighting for a dramatic, moody feel."**

**4. The Photographic Toolset (The "Cinematographer's Eye")**

**This is where you add the final polish that makes an image look like film.\[3]**

**Lens Choice:**

**85mm lens: The classic portrait lens.\[3] Flattering compression and beautiful background blur.**

**50mm lens: A natural, "human eye" perspective. Great for documentary-style realism.\[3]\[4]**

**Anamorphic lens: Adds a widescreen aspect ratio, unique horizontal lens flares, and oval-shaped bokeh for a big-budget movie feel.**

**Film Stock Aesthetic: Mentioning specific films is a shortcut to a complex set of color, contrast, and grain characteristics.**

**Kodak Portra 400: Famous for beautiful, natural skin tones and fine grain.**

**Cinestill 800T: Perfect for night scenes with artificial light, known for its distinct red halation (glow) around lights.\[5]\[6]**

**Kodak Tri-X 400: A classic, gritty black and white film.**

**General Film Terms: Film grain, color grading, cinematic color palette (e.g., teal and orange), movie still.**

**Aspect Ratio: Don't forget to set the frame shape. Add --ar 16:9 or --ar 21:9 at the end of your prompt (for Midjourney) for a wide, cinematic aspect ratio.**

**You are no longer just a portrait photographer; you are now a director blocking a scene, a costume designer, and a cinematographer lighting an entire space.**

**When you pull the camera back, the face becomes smaller, so the burden of storytelling shifts to body language, environment interaction, wardrobe, and overall composition.**

**Here is the expanded guide to prompting for cinematic, realistic characters in wider shots.**

**1. Directing the Body: The "Physical Performance"**

**In a medium or full shot, posture and gesture convey emotion more strongly than facial expression. You must direct the character's physical presence.**

**Replace Emotion with Posture: Instead of saying "a sad man," describe the physical manifestation of sadness.**

**Prompting: "Slumped shoulders, head bowed, hands buried deep in coat pockets."**

**Prompting: "Standing rigid with tension, fists clenched at their sides."**

**Specific Action over Generic Posing: Avoid static poses that look like a fashion catalog. Give them something natural to do.**

**Instead of: "A woman standing on a street."**

**Try: "A woman pausing mid-stride to check her watch on a busy street," or "A mechanic wiping grease from their hands with a rag."**

**The "Unguarded Moment": To achieve realism, aim for candid moments between actions.**

**Keywords: Candid, caught off guard, in motion, slight motion blur on limbs, naturalistic pose.**

**2. Wardrobe as Storytelling: The "Costume Design"**

**Clothing is a massive part of a character's visual identity in wider shots. It needs to look lived-in to be realistic. Imperfection is key.**

**Specific Materials \& Textures: Don't just name the item; name the fabric.**

**Keywords: Heavy wool peacoat, worn denim jacket, faded cotton t-shirt, scuffed leather boots, tweed suit.**

**Evidence of Wear and Use: Clothing in movies rarely looks brand new unless it's a plot point.**

**Keywords: Weathered, threadbare seams, sun-faded, stained, creased, dusty.**

**How it Fits: The drape of clothing adds realism.**

**Prompting: "An oversized coat swallowing their small frame," or "a tailored suit fitting snugly across the shoulders."**

**3. Interaction with the Environment: The "Blocking"**

**A character shouldn't float in front of a background; they must be physically grounded within it.**

**Physical Contact: Make them touch the world around them.**

**Prompting: "Leaning casually against a graffiti-covered brick wall," "sitting hunched over at a crowded diner counter," "kneeling in the dirt examining a plant."**

**Depth and Layers: Place elements between the camera and the character to create depth.**

**Prompting: "Shot through the rain-streaked windshield of a car looking at the character on the street," or "framed by foreground foliage."**

**4. Cinematic Framing for Bodies: The "Wide Shot"**

**You need to tell the AI how much of the body to show and where to place it in the frame.**

**Shot Scale Definitions:**

**Medium Shot (MS): From the waist up. Good for showing body language while still seeing facial reaction.**

**Cowboy Shot (or American Shot): From mid-thigh up. Classic for showing weaponry or tool belts.**

**Full Body Shot (LS): Head to toe. The environment is equally important as the character.**

**Environmental Portrait: The character is relatively small within a vast landscape or detailed room. This emphasizes scale and isolation.**

**Compositional Weight: Where are they in the frame?**

**Prompting: "Character positioned on the far left third of the frame, looking into the negative space on the right."**

**5. Lighting the Full Scene**

**Lighting a whole body in a space is harder than lighting just a face. The light needs to motivate the entire scene.**

**Separation Light: In wider shots, characters can blend into the background. Use rim lighting to pop them out.**

**Prompting: "A strong backlight creating a rim of light around their figure, separating them from the dark alley."**

**Motivated Light Sources: Where is the light coming from in the wide shot?**

**Prompting: "Lit by the warm glow of a streetlamp above them, casting long shadows on the pavement."**

**Atmospheric Lighting: Light interacting with the air adds immense cinematic value.**

**Keywords: Volume light, light rays, haze, fog, dust motes dancing in a shaft of sunlight.**
